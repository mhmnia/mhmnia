About

Measuring how the ground moves, from space.

I am a PhD researcher at the University of Hong Kong, working at the intersection of satellite remote sensing and solid-earth geophysics. My research uses Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR) to detect and interpret subtle ground deformation — uncovering hidden volcanic unrest, mapping rising salt diapirs, and tracking eruptions as they unfold.

I develop new processing methods to push InSAR sensitivity beyond conventional limits, with particular focus on atmospheric correction for small, steep volcanic edifices. I combine these geodetic observations with numerical modeling, petrological constraints, and machine learning to reconstruct subsurface processes from millimeter-scale surface signals.

My recent work on Taftan volcano (Iran) — the first clear unrest signal captured at a long-dormant volcano — received global media coverage in The Independent, New York Post, Daily Mail, and other outlets.

Selected Publications

Peer-reviewed research, 2021 – 2026.

Geophysical Research Letters · 2025

Spontaneous transient summit uplift at Taftan volcano, Iran

First clear unrest signal at a long-dormant volcano along the Makran subduction arc. A novel common-mode filtering technique reveals a 10-month, ~9 cm uplift episode sourced ~500 m beneath the summit.

Bulletin of Volcanology · 2026

Active magmatic processes at the Tibesti intraplate volcanoes

First satellite-based deformation survey of the Tibesti province in the central Sahara. Persistent subsidence at Toussidé–Yirrigué and Soborom points to long-term cooling of mid-crustal magma reservoirs.

Read paper
Geophysical Journal International · 2025

Surface deformation dynamics of Sierra Negra's 2018 eruption

Multi-method study combining InSAR, optical flow, and pixel offset tracking on 6-day Sentinel-1 pairs. Recovers the full eruption cycle and estimates underground magma transfer at ~60 m/hr.

Read paper
Tectonophysics · 2021

Active deformation of salt diapirs in the Kalut basin, Iran

InSAR study of eight salt diapirs in central Iran — one of the most active exposed diapiric systems on Earth. Salt stocks and walls rise at up to 8 mm/yr, with horizontal spread of 1–2 mm/yr along their flanks.
